Why Mahavir Jayanti is celebrated

Mahavir Jayanti is an important religious festival celebrated by Jains worldwide. It marks the birth anniversary of Lord Mahavir, the 24th and last Tirthankara (enlightened being) of Jainism. Tirthankaras are spiritual leaders who have attained enlightenment and provide guidance to followers on the path of liberation. Mahavir Jayanti is observed on the 13th day of the Hindu month of Chaitra (March or April) and is a day of great significance for Jains. The festival is marked by various rituals, prayers, processions, and charitable activities. Devotees visit Jain temples, perform puja (worship), and engage in meditation and spiritual discourses. On this day, Jains strive to follow the teachings of Lord Mahavir, which emphasize non-violence (ahimsa), truthfulness (satya), non-stealing (asteya), chastity (brahmacharya), and non-possessiveness (aparigraha). These principles form the foundation of Jain philosophy and serve as a guide for leading a righteous and ethical life. Mahavir Jayanti is a time of reflection, introspection, and renewal of faith for Jains. It provides an opportunity to remember and honor Lord Mahavir's teachings and his contributions to the spiritual and ethical development of humanity. The festival promotes harmony, compassion, and respect for all living beings, aligning with Jainism's core principles of non-violence and peace.

Ingredients for Mahavir jayanti

Here are some common ingredients used during Mahavir Jayanti puja:

1.water :Water is considered pure and is used for ritual purification and cleansing of the worship area and the deity's idol or image
2.Rice: Rice grains are offered during the puja as a symbol of abundance and prosperity. 3.Diya or oil lamp: Earthen lamps or decorative oil lamps lit with ghee or oil to invoke light and remove darkness.
4.Incense sticks: Fragrant incense sticks are lit to create a pleasant ambiance during the puja.
5.Flowers: Fresh flowers are offered to the deity as a symbol of beauty and devotion.
6.Sweets and fruits: Offerings of sweets like laddoos, pedas, and fruits .
7.Prasadam or sacred food: Cooked food is offered to the deity and then distributed among family members as a blessed offering.

8.Bell: A small bell is rung during the puja to create a sacred sound and mark the beginning and end of the ritual.
9.Sacred water: Water from a sacred river or plain water is used for purification and sprinkling on the devotees.
Sandalwood paste: Sandalwood paste or chandan is used to adorn the deity's idol or image. It has a cooling effect and is considered auspicious.
11.Puja utensils: This includes plates, bowls, spoons, and a small pitcher for performing the rituals.
12.Sacred Thread A sacred thread, called raksha sutra, may be tied on the wrists of devotees as a symbol of protection and blessings.
